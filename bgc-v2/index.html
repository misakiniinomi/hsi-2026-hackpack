About the Club

Rainier Vista Boys & Girls Club

A community anchor in South Seattle, providing after-school and teen programs for youth in one of the city's most diverse neighborhoods.

The Smilow Rainier Vista Boys & Girls Club serves youth and teens in the Rainier Vista neighborhood — a vibrant, diverse community in Southeast Seattle. For many families in this area, the Club represents a safe, affordable, and enriching place for their kids after school.

The Club runs programs in sports, academic support, arts, and leadership — but the community it could serve is much larger than its current membership reflects.

The Challenge

Help more Rainier Valley families discover and join the Club

Today, most families find the Club through word of mouth or random luck. Here's what's making it hard to grow.

  • 📊
    Membership is declining After-school participation is down. Teen enrollment and elementary programs like basketball and flag football have fewer new members than before.
  • 📣
    Word-of-mouth is the main channel — but it doesn't scale Most families hear about the Club from friends or neighbors. There's no reliable way to reach families who don't already know someone who's a member.
  • 🗓️
    Flyers are inconsistent and manual Some clubs distribute flyers at schools, but there's no system — it depends on individual staff motivation and isn't done regularly. Flyers are made in Canva by non-designers.
  • 🖥️
    Enrollment flow causes drop-off Families who get interested sometimes don't complete enrollment. The national signup page is complex, and phone-based alternatives exist but aren't well known.
  • 📱
    Social media is not manageable at local scale One marketing person covers 30+ clubs. Local clubs can't maintain consistent social presence on their own. Individual clubs' events and programs go unannounced.
  • 🏢
    Competitors are visible; the Club is not Other after-school programs in the neighborhood have more visible marketing. Families who are looking for options may not encounter the Club.
Constraints

What teams must work within

These are hard limits — not flexible. Understanding them early will help you build something that could actually be used on Monday.

🔒
National enrollment page cannot be changed The signup URL is managed by Boys & Girls Clubs of America nationally. The local club has no ability to modify it.
✏️
Local web page: limited edits only A local landing page exists and can be edited slightly, but it's within a national template system.
💸
No local marketing budget The Club does not have funds for paid ads, printed materials at scale, or new software subscriptions.
🎨
Canva is the primary design tool Staff are comfortable with Canva. Solutions that require complex design or video production skills won't be sustainable.
☎️
Phone enrollment support is possible Staff can help families enroll by phone — but families need to know this option exists first.
👤
Staff capacity is limited Club staff are focused on running programs. Solutions that require significant ongoing staff time are unlikely to stick.
The sweet spot: Low-cost, low-maintenance solutions that a busy club director or volunteer could actually use — ideally things that get easier over time, not harder.

Current Family Journey

How families find — or fail to find — the Club today

This is the real path a family takes right now. Notice where it falls apart.

1

Rare touchpoint: community event or word of mouth

A small number of families encounter the Club at a local event or hear about it from a friend. This is the primary discovery channel — but it's unpredictable and limited in reach.

2
Friction point

School flyers — inconsistent and unappealing

Some clubs distribute flyers at schools, but coverage is patchy. They're made in Canva by staff who aren't designers, and the quality varies. Many families throw them away or never see them.

3

Family gets interested and looks for more info

If a family gets curious, they may search online or visit the local page. The information available can be hard to navigate or incomplete.

4
Friction point

Enrollment page is confusing or overwhelming

The national enrollment system is designed for all clubs across the country — not optimized for a first-time family in Rainier Valley. Some families give up here. Phone enrollment is available but not well advertised.

5
Structural problem

Social media: one person, 30+ clubs

The national marketing team has one person managing social media for 30+ clubs. Local events and programs rarely get promoted. Each club's unique story goes untold.

6

If a family makes it through: they love it

Members and families who join tend to stay. The Club's programs are genuinely strong — the barrier is reaching and enrolling new families, not retaining them.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to the most common questions from hackathon participants.

Can we change the national enrollment page?
No. The enrollment page at bgca.net is managed nationally by Boys & Girls Clubs of America. The local club has no ability to modify it. Your solution should work around this constraint — not try to replace it.
Can we edit the local Club page?
Partially. There is a local landing page within the national template system. Some elements can be edited. Ask the club contact what's editable — and design your solution with those limits in mind.
Is there a budget for implementation?
Essentially no. There is no local marketing budget. Solutions should rely on free tools (Canva, Google Docs, free web hosting, etc.) or things the Club already has access to. Paid ads, subscriptions, or purchased materials are not feasible.
Who is the primary target audience?
Elementary and middle school youth — and their parents. The Club especially wants to grow participation among K–8 kids, particularly for after-school programs, basketball, and flag football. Parents are often the decision-makers.
Who should be able to use what we build?
Club staff and local leaders — not full-time marketers. If a busy Club director can use your solution in 30 minutes a month, that's ideal. Solutions that require marketing expertise or constant attention won't stick.
Can families enroll by phone?
Yes — but most families don't know this. Staff can support phone-based enrollment. This is an underutilized option that could reduce drop-off for families who find the online process confusing.
What kinds of solutions are NOT welcome?
Don't redesign the enrollment system, propose paid ad campaigns, or build tools requiring ongoing tech staff to maintain. The best solutions are low-cost, low-maintenance, and immediately usable.
What does a great submission look like?
Small scope, real impact, actually usable. A Canva template pack the Club can use next week beats a complex app that requires months of development. We want ideas that are grounded in the real constraints and could be put into action.
